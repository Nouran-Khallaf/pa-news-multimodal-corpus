# Research-question and method map

| RQ | Evidence | Method | Validation | Establishes | Does not establish |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| RQ1. Which lexical and semantic themes distinguish this news corpus from a genre- and time-matched UK political-news comparator? | Target and comparator documents | Frequency, document frequency, log-likelihood, log-ratio, concordance review | Multiple-testing correction and manual concordance sample | Relative lexical distinctiveness under the sampling design | Ideological importance, intent, or coded meaning |
| RQ2. How are named actors and institutions represented? | Sentences containing named entities | NER, sentence co-occurrence, dependency-based predication candidates, collocations | Manual correction of entities and coded sample of predications | Recurrent textual associations and candidate descriptions | Real-world organisational links or beliefs of individuals |
| RQ3. Which frames and legitimation strategies recur, and how do they change over time? | Sentence-level candidate passages and dates | Lexicon retrieval, optional NLI scoring, human discourse coding, normalised diachronic frequencies | Two-coder sample, codebook revision, adjudication | Distribution of validated textual frames | Author intention, audience effects, or causal impact |
| RQ4. What functions do images perform in relation to article text? | Article–image pairs, captions, OCR and dates | Visual clustering, provisional visual categories, generated captions, OCR, SigLIP similarity, human multimodal coding | Stratified manual review and model error analysis | Patterns of visual self-presentation, evidence, mobilisation and text–image relations | Identity, ethnicity, political affiliation, truthfulness or emotional response |
| RQ5. Which terms merit investigation as potentially coded or strategically ambiguous language? | Key terms, collocations and concordance contexts | Candidate queue followed by close reading and comparator checks | Independent review with explicit ambiguity and alternative readings | Corpus-evidenced candidates and their contextual functions | That any term is inherently a dog whistle |
